The Timeline

Three years that changed cinema forever

1964
A Fistful of Dollars Per un pugno di dollari

The one that started it all. An unofficial remake of Akira Kurosawa's Yojimbo, shot in Spain on a budget of $200,000. Clint Eastwood, a TV actor from Rawhide, was cast only after Charles Bronson and Henry Fonda turned it down. The film introduced the world to the "Man with No Name" — a cynical, poncho-wearing drifter who plays two rival gangs against each other in the border town of San Miguel. The close-up cinematography, long silences, and Morricone's whistle-driven score rewrote the rules of the western. It grossed $14.5 million — 72x its budget — and launched the spaghetti western explosion.

1965
For a Few Dollars More Per qualche dollaro in più

The one that perfected the formula. Leone doubled the budget to $600,000 and introduced a second protagonist: Colonel Douglas Mortimer (Lee Van Cleef), a precision bounty hunter with a dark past. The film pairs Eastwood's "Manco" with Van Cleef's Mortimer in an uneasy alliance to take down the drug-addled bandit El Indio (Gian Maria Volontè). This is where Leone's operatic style crystallized — the pocket watch musical duel, the church shootout, the moral ambiguity. The chemistry between Eastwood and Van Cleef was electric, and Leone realized the trilogy needed a third force: pure, chaotic evil.

1966
The Good, the Bad and the Ugly Il buono, il brutto, il cattivo

The masterpiece. With $1.2 million and an epic scope, Leone delivered what many consider the greatest western ever made. Set during the Civil War, three gunslingers — Blondie (the Good), Angel Eyes (the Bad), and Tuco (the Ugly) — hunt for $200,000 in Confederate gold buried in a grave at Sad Hill Cemetery. Each knows only part of the secret. The film is a meditation on greed, violence, and the absurd theater of war. Its climax — the three-way Mexican standoff at Sad Hill, intercut with extreme close-ups, scored to Morricone's "The Ecstasy of Gold" — is arguably the most iconic sequence in cinema history. The film earned $25 million and cemented Leone as a master.

Behind the Scenes

The Sergio Leone story

01

Sergio Leone Had Never Directed a Western

Before A Fistful of Dollars, Leone had only directed The Colossus of Rhodes (1961), a sword-and-sandal epic. He reinvented the western without having made one. He watched Kurosawa's Yojimbo and thought: "This is a western." He was right. He shot in Spain's Tabernas Desert because it looked more like the American West than America did.

Origins
02

Clint Eastwood Was Paid $15,000

For A Fistful of Dollars, Eastwood earned $15,000 — less than the cost of his poncho in today's collector market. He took the role because he was tired of playing the clean-cut cowboy on Rawhide. By The Good, the Bad and the Ugly, his salary had jumped to $250,000 plus 10% of the profits. The cigar was his idea — he hated the stubby cigarillos Leone wanted.

Cast
03

The Bridge Explosion Was Done Twice

The iconic bridge explosion in The Good, the Bad and the Ugly was so expensive to set up that Leone couldn't afford a second take. But a Spanish army captain, eager to impress a visiting dignitary, triggered the explosives early — destroying the bridge before cameras rolled. Leone convinced the Spanish army to rebuild it, and the second explosion made it into the film.

Production
04

Leone Spoke Almost No English

Despite directing American actors in English-language films, Sergio Leone spoke very little English. He communicated through a translator, gestures, and sheer force of will. Eastwood recalled that Leone would act out entire scenes physically, his massive frame gesticulating wildly, shouting in Italian. The actors performed in English but were dubbed in post-production — standard for Italian cinema.

Direction
05

The Sad Hill Cemetery Was Built by Spanish Soldiers

The famous circular cemetery with 5,000 graves in the final showdown took two days to build by 250 Spanish soldiers. Today, a dedicated group of fans (the Sad Hill Cultural Association) has restored the site in northern Spain. You can visit it, stand where Blondie stood, and feel the weight of cinema history. The grave marked "Unknown" next to Arch Stanton's still reads "UNKNOWN."

Locations
06

Eli Wallach Nearly Died Three Times

During filming, Wallach was nearly decapitated when the horse-riding noose scene went wrong (the horse bolted with a noose around his neck). He was nearly poisoned when Leone used acid in his food to make his face contort on camera. And he nearly drowned crossing a river. Wallach later quipped: "The only thing I didn't do with Sergio was die." He lived to 98.

Danger
07

Ennio Morricone Scored Without Seeing the Film

Leone would describe scenes to Morricone, who composed the music beforehand. The actors then performed to the playback of Morricone's score on set. This reversed the traditional process and explains why the music and imagery are so perfectly fused — the film was built around the score, not the other way around. The coyote howl in the main theme is a human voice.

Music
08

The Trilogy Wasn't Originally a Trilogy

Leone never intended to make a trilogy. A Fistful of Dollars was a standalone film. Its massive success prompted For a Few Dollars More. Only after that did Leone conceive of the epic finale. The films were marketed as a trilogy retroactively, and the "Man with No Name" designation was an American marketing invention — in the Italian originals, the characters have names (Joe, Manco, Blondie).

Trivia

Ennio Morricone

1928 — 2020

The sound of the West wasn't born in Texas. It was born in Rome, in the mind of a classically trained composer who dared to use electric guitars, whistles, whip cracks, gunshots, and human voices as instruments. Morricone scored over 500 films in his career, but the Dollars Trilogy remains his signature. His music didn't accompany the action — it was the action. Every close-up, every standoff, every gallop across the desert moved to his rhythm. He won an Oscar at 87 for The Hateful Eight — a career fittingly bookended by a western.
